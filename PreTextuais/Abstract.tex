\begin{resumo}[Abstract]
 \begin{otherlanguage*}{english}
  This study investigates systematic divergences in economic perceptions between the general public and economists with graduate degrees in Brazil, examining the extent to which these divergences are associated with self-reported ideological position and advanced economic training. The research design combines (i) an adaptation of the Survey of Americans and Economists on the Economy (SAEE) to the Brazilian context and (ii) estimation of 53 ordered logit models with demographic, socioeconomic, and ideological controls. The sample comprises $n = 184$ respondents; the effective N in models is approximately 146 after excluding the 38 respondents without a declared ideological position. The group of interest includes $n = 17$ economists with graduate degrees (9\% of the sample). Results indicate that the political spectrum is associated with systematic differences in 34 of the 53 dependent variables after Benjamini-Hochberg correction; advanced economic training presents five nominally significant associations (all with direction consistent with the literature), none surviving multiple-comparison correction --- a predictable outcome given the statistical power of 28\% with $n_{\text{econ}} = 17$. The study is exploratory in character; the results are conditional associations within the sample frame, not causal effects nor representative estimates for the Brazilian population.
   \textbf{Keywords}: behavioral economics; economic perception; public opinion; economic literacy; political spectrum.
 \end{otherlanguage*}
\end{resumo}
